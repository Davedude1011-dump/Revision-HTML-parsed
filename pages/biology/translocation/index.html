This intricate mechanism ensures a balanced distribution of energy and nutrients throughout the plant. Let's delve into the key aspects of translocation:</div> <div class="header">1. **Phloem Transport System**</div> <div class="text">Translocation predominantly occurs through the phloem, a specialized vascular tissue responsible for the transport of organic compounds. The primary driving force behind this process is the difference in pressure between source and sink areas.</div> <div class="header">2. **Source and Sink Organs**</div> <div class="text">In translocation, leaves, particularly those with high photosynthetic activity, serve as source organs where glucose is produced through photosynthesis. The glucose, along with other assimilates, is then actively transported to sink organs such as roots, developing fruits, and storage tissues where it is utilized or stored for future use.</div> <div class="header">3. **Loading and Unloading of Phloem Sieve Tubes**</div> <div class="text">Loading of assimilates into the phloem sieve tubes occurs in source organs. Here, sugars are actively transported into the sieve tubes, creating a high concentration of solutes. This high concentration generates osmotic pressure, causing water to enter the sieve tubes from xylem vessels. At the sink organs, unloading of assimilates occurs, facilitated by active transport and diffusion to ensure proper distribution.</div> <div class="header">4. **Mass Flow Hypothesis**</div> <div class="text">The Mass Flow Hypothesis is a widely accepted theory explaining the movement of sap in the phloem.
