Understanding how vaccines work provides insights into the principles of immunization and the prevention of infectious diseases.</div> <div class="header">1. Introduction to Vaccines</div> <div class="text">- **Purpose:** Vaccines are biological preparations that stimulate the immune system to recognize and remember specific pathogens, preventing or mitigating future infections.</div> <div class="text">- **Components:** Vaccines may contain weakened or inactivated forms of pathogens, fragments of pathogens, or synthetic substances resembling pathogen components (antigens).</div> <div class="header">2. Mechanism of Action</div> <div class="text">- **Antigen Presentation:** Vaccines introduce antigens from pathogens without causing the disease. Antigen-presenting cells (APCs) process these antigens and present them to T cells, initiating an immune response.</div> <div class="text">- **B Cell Activation:** B cells recognize the antigens and produce antibodies specific to the pathogen. Memory B cells are generated to provide long-term immunity.</div> <div class="text">- **T Cell Activation:** Helper T cells assist in activating B cells and cytotoxic T cells, coordinating immune responses. Memory T cells contribute to immunological memory.</div> <div class="header">3. Types of Vaccines</div> <div class="text">- **Live Attenuated Vaccines:** Weakened forms of live pathogens, providing strong and long-lasting immunity. Examples include measles, mumps, and rubella (MMR) vaccine.</div> <div class="text">- **Inactivated Vaccines:** Use killed pathogens or components to stimulate an immune response. Examples include the polio vaccine.</div> <div class="text">- **Subunit, Recombinant, or Conjugate Vaccines:** Utilize specific components of pathogens, such as proteins or sugars, to induce an immune response. Examples include the hepatitis B vaccine.</div> <div class="header">4. Importance of Vaccination</div> <div class="text">- **Disease Prevention:** Vaccination prevents the spread of infectious diseases, protecting individuals and communities.</div> <div class="text">- **Herd Immunity:** When a significant portion of a population is vaccinated, it provides indirect protection to unvaccinated individuals by limiting the spread of pathogens.</div> <div class="header">5.
