<div class="main-block"> <div class="buttons"> <buttons class="back"></buttons> <buttons class="forward"></buttons> </div> <xmp> <div class="subject">Chemistry</div> <div class="title">Half Equations</div> <br> <div class="date">05/01/24</div> <br><br><br> <div class="header">Half Equations in Chemistry</div> <div class="text">In chemistry, half equations are used to represent the oxidation or reduction processes that occur during redox reactions. These equations focus on either the reduction (gain of electrons) or oxidation (loss of electrons) half of the reaction.</div> <div class="header">Oxidation Half Equation</div> <div class="text">The oxidation half equation describes the process in which a species loses electrons. It is written by showing the reactant losing electrons and forming products. For example:</div> <div class="text">\[ \text{Zn} \rightarrow \text{Zn}^{2+} + 2e^- \]</div> <div class="text">In this equation, zinc (\( \text{Zn} \)) undergoes oxidation, losing two electrons to form \( \text{Zn}^{2+} \).</div> <div class="header">Reduction Half Equation</div> <div class="text">Conversely, the reduction half equation illustrates the gain of electrons by a species. It is written by showing the reactant gaining electrons and forming products. For example:</div> <div class="text">\[ \text{Cu}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu} \]</div> <div class="text">In this equation, copper ions (\( \text{Cu}^{2+} \)) undergo reduction, gaining two electrons to form copper (\( \text{Cu} \)).</div> <div class="header">Balancing Half Equations</div> <div class="text">It's crucial to ensure that the number of electrons lost in the oxidation half equation equals the number gained in the reduction half equation. This balance is necessary for the overall redox reaction to occur smoothly.</div> <div class="header">Net Ionic Equation</div> <div class="text">The net ionic equation is obtained by adding the balanced oxidation and reduction half equations, canceling out the electrons produced or consumed in the process.
