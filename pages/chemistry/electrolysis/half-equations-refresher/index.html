<div class="subject">Chemistry</div> <div class="title">Half Equations</div> <br> <div class="date">12/01/24</div> <br><br><br> <div class="header">Half Equations Refresher</div> <div class="text">In chemical reactions, half equations are essential for expressing the changes that occur at the electrodes during processes like electrolysis. Let's delve into the fundamentals of half equations and explore examples across different scenarios.</div> <div class="header">1. Definition of Half Equations</div> <div class="text">- **Half Equation Form:** A half equation represents either the oxidation or reduction half of a redox reaction. The oxidation half equation shows the loss of electrons, while the reduction half equation depicts the gain of electrons.</div> <div class="text">- **Electron Representation:** Electrons are denoted as \(e^-\) in half equations, and the overall charge on both sides of the equation must balance.</div> <div class="header">2. Oxidation Half Equation</div> <div class="text">- **Example:** For the oxidation of iron (Fe) to iron(II) ions (Fe²⁺), the half equation is: \(Fe(s) \rightarrow Fe^{2+}(aq) + 2e^-\).</div> <div class="text">- **Explanation:** Iron loses electrons to form iron(II) ions, and the electrons are represented on the product side of the equation.</div> <div class="header">3. Reduction Half Equation</div> <div class="text">- **Example:** In the reduction of copper(II) ions (Cu²⁺) to copper (Cu), the half equation is: \(Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)\).</div> <div class="text">- **Explanation:** Copper(II) ions gain electrons to form solid copper, and the electrons appear on the reactant side of the equation.</div> <div class="header">4. Balancing Half Equations</div> <div class="text">- **Rule:** The number of electrons lost in the oxidation half equation must equal the number gained in the reduction half equation for the reaction to be balanced.</div> <div class="text">- **Example:** Balancing the half equations for the reaction between zinc and copper(II) ions: \(Zn(s) + Cu^{2+}(aq) \rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq) + Cu(s)\).</div> <div class="header">5. Ionic Half Equations in Solution</div> <div class="text">- **Example:** In the electrolysis of water, the reduction half equation for the formation of hydroxide ions is: \(2H₂O(l) + 2e^- \rightarrow H₂(g) + 2OH^-(aq)\).</div> <div class="text">- **Explanation:** Water is reduced to produce hydrogen gas and hydroxide ions.</div> <div class="header">Conclusion</div> <div class="text">Mastering half equations is crucial for understanding redox reactions, especially in the context of electrolysis.
