Ionization of Water</div> <div class="text">- **Dissociation:** Water molecules naturally undergo ionization, breaking into hydrogen ions (H⁺) and hydroxide ions (OH⁻). This ionization contributes to the availability of ions in the electrolyte.</div> <div class="header">3. Electrical Conductivity</div> <div class="text">- **Facilitation of Current:** Water, with its ionized form, enhances the electrical conductivity of the electrolyte. This facilitates the flow of current between the electrodes during electrolysis.</div> <div class="header">4. Hydroxide Ion Production</div> <div class="text">- **OH⁻ Ions:** The production of hydroxide ions is a result of water reduction at the cathode. These hydroxide ions contribute to the alkaline nature of the solution around the cathode.</div> <div class="header">5. pH Changes</div> <div class="text">- **Acidic and Basic Zones:** The electrolysis of water leads to pH changes in the solution. The region around the anode becomes acidic due to the release of H⁺ ions, while the cathode region becomes basic due to the production of OH⁻ ions.</div> <div class="header">6. Role in Other Electrolytic Processes</div> <div class="text">- **Widespread Use:** Water is a common electrolyte in various electrochemical processes, including the electrolysis of salts and the production of chlorine, sodium hydroxide, and hydrogen.</div> <div class="header">Conclusion</div> <div class="text">Water's involvement in electrolysis extends beyond being a passive medium.
