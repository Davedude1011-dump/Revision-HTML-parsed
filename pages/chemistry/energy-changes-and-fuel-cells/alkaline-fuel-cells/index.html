They offer several advantages over other fuel cell technologies and have been used in various applications. Here's an overview of alkaline fuel cells:</div> <div class="header">1. Operating Principle</div> <div class="text">- **Electrolyte:** AFCs use an alkaline electrolyte, typically potassium hydroxide (KOH) solution, which conducts ions between the electrodes while also facilitating the chemical reactions.</div> <div class="text">- **Electrodes:** The electrodes in an AFC are typically made of porous carbon materials, such as graphite, coated with catalysts, often platinum, to enhance the electrochemical reactions.</div> <div class="text">- **Anode Reaction:** At the anode, hydrogen gas (H₂) is oxidized to produce protons (H⁺) and electrons (e⁻).</div> <div class="text">- **Cathode Reaction:** At the cathode, oxygen gas (O₂) reacts with protons and electrons to form water (H₂O).</div> <div class="text">- **Overall Reaction:** The overall chemical reaction in an AFC is the combination of the anode and cathode reactions, resulting in the production of electricity and water as by-products.</div> <div class="header">2. Advantages</div> <div class="text">- **High Efficiency:** AFCs have relatively high efficiency in converting chemical energy into electricity, making them suitable for various applications where energy efficiency is critical.</div> <div class="text">- **Fast Start-up:** AFCs can be quickly started and brought to full operating capacity, making them suitable for applications requiring rapid power generation.</div> <div class="text">- **Low Emissions:** The only by-product of the AFC reaction is water, making them environmentally friendly and suitable for applications requiring low emissions.</div> <div class="header">3. Applications</div> <div class="text">- **Space Exploration:** AFCs have been used in space missions by space agencies such as NASA due to their reliability, high efficiency, and ability to operate in extreme conditions.</div> <div class="text">- **Transportation:** While not as common as other fuel cell technologies, AFCs have been explored for use in transportation applications, including buses and submarines.</div> <div class="text">- **Stationary Power Generation:** AFCs can be used for stationary power generation in remote locations or as backup power sources due to their reliability and fast start-up times.</div> <div class="header">4. Challenges</div> <div class="text">- **Cost:** AFCs typically use expensive catalysts such as platinum, which can contribute to high system costs compared to other fuel cell technologies.</div> <div class="text">- **Durability:** AFCs may experience degradation over time, particularly at the electrodes and electrolyte, which can affect performance and longevity.</div> <div class="text">- **Fuel Handling:** Like other hydrogen-based fuel cells, AFCs require infrastructure for hydrogen storage, distribution, and refueling, which can present logistical challenges.</div> <div class="header">Conclusion</div> <div class="text">Alkaline fuel cells offer a promising technology for clean and efficient energy conversion, with applications ranging from space exploration to stationary power generation.
