Here's an overview of the anode and cathode processes in fuel cells:</div> <div class="header">1. Anode Process</div> <div class="text">- **Oxidation:** At the anode, the fuel undergoes oxidation. In hydrogen fuel cells, molecular hydrogen (H₂) is typically used as the fuel.</div> <div class="text">- **Electron Release:** When hydrogen molecules encounter the catalyst-coated anode surface, they dissociate into protons (H⁺) and electrons (e⁻).</div> <div class="text">- **Proton Migration:** Protons generated at the anode move through the electrolyte towards the cathode.</div> <div class="text">- **Electron Flow:** Electrons released during the oxidation reaction travel through an external circuit, creating an electric current that can be harnessed to perform work.</div> <div class="header">2. Cathode Process</div> <div class="text">- **Reduction:** At the cathode, the oxidant (typically oxygen or air) undergoes reduction. Oxygen molecules (O₂) from the air are commonly used in fuel cells.</div> <div class="text">- **Proton Reception:** Protons migrating through the electrolyte from the anode combine with oxygen molecules and electrons arriving from the external circuit at the cathode.</div> <div class="text">- **Formation of Water:** The combination of protons, electrons, and oxygen molecules at the cathode results in the formation of water (H₂O) as a by-product.</div> <div class="text">- **Completing the Circuit:** The water produced at the cathode is typically removed from the fuel cell to prevent flooding and ensure efficient operation.</div> <div class="header">Overall Reaction</div> <div class="text">The overall chemical reaction in a fuel cell involves the combination of the anode and cathode processes:</div> <div class="text">- **Anode Reaction:** H₂ ⟶ 2H⁺ + 2e⁻</div> <div class="text">- **Cathode Reaction:** ½O₂ + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ ⟶ H₂O</div> <div class="text">- **Overall Reaction:** H₂ + ½O₂ ⟶ H₂O</div> <div class="header">Conclusion</div> <div class="text">Anode and cathode processes in fuel cells are essential for converting chemical energy into electricity through controlled electrochemical reactions.
