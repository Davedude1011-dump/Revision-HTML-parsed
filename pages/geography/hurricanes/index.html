Hurricanes form over tropical and subtropical oceans where these warm conditions prevail.</div> <div class="header">Atmospheric Instability</div> <div class="text">Hurricanes thrive in an atmosphere that exhibits instability. This means that as warm, moist air rises from the ocean's surface, it cools and condenses, releasing latent heat into the atmosphere. This process further warms the air, causing it to rise even more rapidly. The rising warm air creates low-pressure systems, which are essential for hurricane development.</div> <div class="header">Low-Pressure Systems</div> <div class="text">Low-pressure systems serve as the organizing centers of hurricanes. As warm, moist air rises and creates areas of low pressure at the surface, surrounding air rushes in to fill the void, creating a circular pattern of air movement. This rotation imparts the characteristic spiral structure to the storm and plays a crucial role in its development.</div> <div class="header">Moisture in the Atmosphere</div> <div class="text">A hurricane's moisture supply is essential for its sustenance. The release of heat during condensation helps maintain the low-pressure system at the hurricane's center.</div> <div class="header">Absence of Wind Shear</div> <div class="text">Wind shear, the change in wind speed and direction with altitude, can disrupt the organization of a developing hurricane. For a hurricane to form and strengthen, it requires minimal wind shear in the upper atmosphere. Strong wind shear can tilt the storm and disrupt its vertical structure, inhibiting its ability to intensify.</div> <div class="header">Formation and Lifecycle</div> <div class="text">Hurricanes typically form over warm ocean waters and begin as tropical disturbances. As they gain strength, they progress through stages, including tropical depression, tropical storm, and hurricane.
