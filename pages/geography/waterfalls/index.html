River Erosion</div> <div class="text">The formation of a waterfall typically begins with river erosion. Rivers flow over landscapes, gradually wearing away rocks and sediment through processes such as abrasion, hydraulic action, and corrosion. Over time, these erosional forces create irregularities in the riverbed.</div> <div class="header">2. Differential Erosion</div> <div class="text">Differential erosion occurs when certain rock formations are more resistant to erosion than others. In a river's course, some areas may consist of harder rock, creating elevated steps or ledges. These resistant rock layers act as natural barriers against erosion, while the surrounding rocks are more easily worn away.</div> <div class="header">3. Formation of a Steep Drop</div> <div class="text">As the river continues its course, the areas of softer rock erode more rapidly, leaving behind a steep drop or cliff. Plunge Pool Formation</div> <div class="text">The water flowing over the edge of the steep drop creates a plunge pool at the base of the waterfall. The impact of the falling water, combined with any debris it carries, further erodes the riverbed at the base, excavating a deep pool. This plunge pool is a key characteristic of waterfall morphology.</div> <div class="header">5. Continued Retreat</div> <div class="text">The process of erosion and retreat continues over time. The waterfall's base recedes upstream as the river erodes the rock and sediment. This ongoing retreat contributes to the distinctive appearance of a waterfall, with its cascading water over a vertical or near-vertical drop.</div> <div class="header">6. Characteristics of Waterfalls</div> <div class="text">Waterfalls exhibit various characteristics, including their height, width, and the volume of water cascading over them.
